The Other Front: Russia's Assault on Ukraine's Healthcare

In five years of full-scale war, Russia has attacked Ukraine's healthcare system nearly 3,000 times, hitting facilities, workers, and medical supplies across the country. The World Bank estimates the damage will cost more than $19 billion to repair.

by Luiza Swierzawska  ·  April 12, 2026

The Russian invasion has inflicted unprecedented damage on Ukraine's healthcare system — striking healthcare workers, facilities, ambulances, and medical warehouses across the country. Over the past five years, Russian attacks have driven Ukraine's healthcare infrastructure into a crisis that will cost billions of dollars to recover from. Data collected on these attacks by the World Health Organization (WHO) shows no signs that the destruction is slowing.

Since the war began, which on February 24 entered its fifth year, the WHO has tracked attacks on healthcare in Ukraine. As of April 2026, the database had registered over 2,900. Nearly half occurred in the first year of the invasion, but 2025 recorded the second-highest total, with attacks rising 20% from the year before.

According to World Bank estimates, recovering from these losses would require more than $19.4 billion.

But it is not only physical reconstruction that Ukraine faces. The ongoing pressure on the healthcare system is twofold. Rising rates of war-related trauma have intensified demand across the full spectrum of medical response — from surgery and blood products to mental health care, infection control, and rehabilitation. But these needs are increasingly difficult to meet in a country where healthcare infrastructure has itself become a target. The WHO notes that the crisis is compounded by the "cascading effects of strikes on civilian infrastructure, including thermal power plants that underpin the country's power grid."

WHO classifies attacks into several categories, which frequently occur in combination. The most common type, by far, is violence with heavy weapons.

Violence with heavy weapons

Violence with heavy weapons — including firearms, tanks, missiles, bombs, and mortars — has been documented 2,655 times since the start of the war. This figure shows that attacks on healthcare are not rarely isolated incidents but part of broader military operations.

Removal of health care assets

In 225 cases, the target was the removal of healthcare assets — including transport, supplies, and materials. In one documented case, the Mariupol City Council reported that around 60 ventilators had been stolen from city hospitals, equipment that was critical at the height of the Covid pandemic. Intentional attacks on healthcare infrastructure are violations of the laws of war under the Geneva Convention.

Militarization of a health care asset

In 103 cases, the WHO recorded militarization of a healthcare asset — when military forces use a medical facility for non-medical purposes, such as a command post, barracks, or weapons storage site. Under international humanitarian law, this strips the facility of its protected status.

Violence with individual weapons

Violence with individual weapons, recorded 70 times, involves the use of knives, bricks, clubs, guns, grenades, and improvised explosive devices (IEDs).

Psychological violence, threat of violence, intimidation

The WHO recorded 60 cases of psychological violence, threats, and intimidation directed at healthcare workers or patients.

Obstruction to health care delivery

In 56 cases, attacks were classified as obstruction of healthcare delivery — a category that can take physical, administrative, or legal forms.

Others

The remaining attacks fall into smaller or unclassified categories: 40 cases where the nature of the attack is unknown, plus armed searches of medical personnel (19), abductions or detentions of health workers and patients (19), assaults without weapons (4), arson (3), and one unspecified incident.

Those living in the rural areas are the most impacted. Fuel shortages and damaged roads only worsen the access to remaining public health services.

Now Ukrainians are enduring the aftermath of the coldest winters the country has seen in over a decade.

This is not the first time that Ukraine has faced attacks on their healthcare infrastructure by Russia. Habicht et al. point out that Ukraine implemented a package of reforms to their healthcare system following the Russian invasion of Crimea in 2014 and in anticipation of future attacks. Radical changes were made when it comes to delivering and financing care such as tying payments to actual services rather than aging infrastructure, and guaranteeing every resident access to a family doctor free of charge. By February 2022, 32.5 million Ukrainians — 82% of the population — were enrolled. When the invasion began, those systems proved unexpectedly resilient.
