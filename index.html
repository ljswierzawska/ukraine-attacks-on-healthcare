The Other Front: Russia's Assault on Ukraine's Healthcare

Since Russia's full-scale invasion, WHO has documented nearly 3,000 attacks on Ukraine's healthcare system. These attacks have accumulated $19 billion in damages, and the human and financial toll just keeps growing.

By Luiza Swierzawska

The Russian invasion has inflicted unprecedented damage on Ukraine's healthcare system — striking health workers, facilities, ambulances, and medical warehouses across the country. Over the past five years, Russian attacks have driven Ukraine's healthcare infrastructure into a crisis that will cost billions of dollars to recover from. The data shows no signs that the destruction is slowing.

Since the war began, which on February 24 entered its fifth year, the WHO has tracked attacks on healthcare in Ukraine. As of April 2026, the database had registered over 2,900. Nearly half occurred in the first year of the invasion, but 2025 recorded the second-highest total, with attacks rising 20% from the year before.

According to World Bank estimates, recovering from these losses would require more than $19.4 billion.

The pressure on the healthcare system is twofold. Rising rates of war-related trauma have intensified demand across the full spectrum of medical response — from surgery and blood products to mental health care, infection control, and rehabilitation. But these needs are increasingly difficult to meet in a country where healthcare infrastructure has itself become a target. WHO notes that the crisis is compounded by the "cascading effects of strikes on civilian infrastructure, including thermal power plants that underpin the country's power grid."

WHO classifies attacks into several categories, which often occur in combination. The most common type, by far, is violence with heavy weapons.

Violence with heavy weapons — 2,655

Violence with heavy weapons — including firearms, tanks, missiles, bombs, and mortars — is the most frequently recorded category. Documented 2,655 times since the start of the war, the figure points to healthcare attacks being rarely isolated incidents but part of broader military operations.

Removal of health care assets — 225

In 225 cases, the target was the removal of healthcare assets — including transport, supplies, and materials. In one documented case, the Mariupol City Council reported that around 60 ventilators had been stolen from city hospitals, equipment that was critical at the height of the Covid pandemic.

Militarization of a health care asset — 103

In 103 cases, WHO recorded militarization of a healthcare asset — when military forces use a medical facility for non-medical purposes, such as a command post, barracks, or weapons storage site. Under international humanitarian law, this strips the facility of its protected status.

Violence with individual weapons — 70

Violence with individual weapons, recorded 70 times, involves the use of knives, bricks, clubs, guns, grenades, and improvised explosive devices (IEDs).

Psychological violence, threat of violence, intimidation — 60

WHO recorded 60 cases of psychological violence, threats, and intimidation directed at healthcare workers or patients.

Obstruction to health care delivery — 56

In 56 cases, attacks were classified as obstruction of healthcare delivery — a category that can take physical, administrative, or legal forms.

Others

The remaining attacks fall into smaller or unclassified categories: 40 cases where the nature of the attack is unknown, plus armed searches of medical personnel (19), abductions or detentions of health workers and patients (19), assaults without weapons (4), arson (3), and one unspecified incident.
